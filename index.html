About

My name is Andrew Foote. I was born in Liverpool in 1995, and have lived in Rainhill most of my life. From 2013 to 2016 I was at the University of York studying Linguistics and Mathematics. After graduating I spent the next year studying for a master's degree in Evolution of Language and Cognition at the University of Edinburgh, and graduated in December.

I am interested especially in mathematics, and in various disciplines which I think of as related, namely biology, linguistics, anthropology, sociology and history, in that they are about describing and understanding variation (in language grammars, organisms, cultures, ways of organizing society, etc.)

The links below showcase some of the things I've written or made relating to my interests which I think are most worthy of attention. If you click on the individual section headers, you'll be taken to a page dedicated specifically to that subject, which may contain links to other, less interesting works of mine.

Mathematics

The size of the set of the badly approximable numbers. My final year project for my undergraduate degree, completed in early 2016. It's a work of mathematical exposition on the subject of Diophantine approximation, which develops the basic theory to the point that it can be understood what the “badly approximable” numbers are, and then proves two results relating to how many badly approximable numbers there are: (a) there are uncountably many, but (b) they make up a set of Lebesgue measure 0.

A nonstandard construction of the real numbers. An article written during June 2018, explaining how to construct the real numbers using nonstandard analysis.

Linguistics

My conlangs for the Akana collaborative conworld: Ìletlégbàku (spring 2010) and Wendoth (v. 1.0, summer 2010–winter 2011; v. 2.0, unfinished, spring 2015).

Noun classes across different languages. An essay I wrote as an exercise at the end of Year 12 in 2012 on various aspects of noun classes as a linguistic phenomenon: what sort of noun classes there are, how common they are, what role they serve, etc.

Biology

My bird checklist. Contains all the birds that regularly occur in the British Isles, including scarce migrants (but not vagrants), plus lots of additional data (e.g. rarity data from BirdTrack).

My birdsong player. I'm not very good at identifying birds from their song, so I made this to help me—it's a little radio that will play the songs of birds that inhabit the British Isles, and will give you a notification when the next song plays even if you're on another tab, so that you always know which bird's song is being played.

My collection of pictures of organisms. I like to go out looking for wildlife and to take pictures of it, and to keep track of which species I still haven't yet managed to see. This page is where I put the pictures. It's organized by phylogeny, so it also works as a checklist.

The evolution of the domestic dog. An essay I wrote as an exercise at the end of Year 12 in 2012 on the evolution of the domestic dog, and what its wild ancestor might be (I think a lot of new research has been done on this topic recently—maybe I will revise the essay one day!)
